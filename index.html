Background of Muller's Method

Research and Extraction by SingJie Srisoi

What is the root-finding problem?

The root-finding problem is a fundamental mathematical and algorithmic challenge in numerical analysis. Given a continuous function f(x), the goal is to determine a value x = c, called the root or zero of the function such that:
f(c) = 0
Geometrically, finding a root corresponds to identifying the exact point where the curve of the function intersects or touches the x-axis.

Categorization of Roots

  1. Real Roots: The values of x where the function crosses the real number line x-axis.
  2. Complex Roots: The values involving imaginary numbers where the function does not intersect the real x-axis, but possesses solutions in the complex plane.

Analytical vs. Numerical Approaches

  • Analytical Solutions: Simple algebraic functions can be solved exactly using traditional algebraic formulas.
  • Numerical Solutions: Higher-degree polynomials (degree more than and equal to 5) and transcendental functions cannot be solved using exact algebraic formulas. Therefore, we must rely on iterative numerical methods to approximate the roots within a specific margin of error.

History of Root-Finding Problem

The evolution of root-finding spans millennia, tracking a major structural shift from exact geometric formulas to advanced computational approximations.

Ancient and Medieval Foundations (c. 1800 BCE - 1500 CE) The Babylonians developed some of the earliest recorded numerical workflows, using primitive arithmetic to iteratively compute square roots. Early Asian and Mediterranean mathematics utilized basic linear interpolation techniques, such as the Method of False Position (Regula Falsi).
The Algebraic Barrier (16th - 19th Century) During the Italian Renaissance, mathematicians achieved breakthroughs with algebraic formulas to solve cubic and quartic equations. However, Niels Henrik Abel and Évariste Galois rigorously proved via the Abel-Ruffini Theorem (1824) that it is logically impossible to solve polynomials of degree 5 or higher using standard radicals. This historic barrier permanently shifted mathematics toward constructing iterative numerical approximations.
The Calculus and Modern Computing Era Sir Isaac Newton and Joseph Raphson integrated differential calculus, creating the Newton-Raphson Method using tangent lines to converge rapidly on a root. The Secant Method arose as a derivative-free alternative, drawing straight lines through two initial data points.
David Muller and the Birth of Muller's Method (1956) In 1956, American mathematician David E. Muller published a major breakthrough. Instead of drawing a straight line through two points like the Secant Method, Muller's Method fits a quadratic parabola through three points. It established two major mathematical advantages: superlinear convergence (rate of approx. 1.84) and automated discovery of complex roots via negative discriminants.

Real-World Application of Root-Finding

1. Finance (Internal Rate of Return) To determine the Internal Rate of Return (IRR), financial analysts must find the exact discount rate r that forces the Net Present Value (NPV) to equal zero:
NPV = ∑t=0n Ct (1 + r)t = 0
2. Fluid Dynamics (Colebrook Equation) Determining the turbulent fluid friction factor f requires solving the implicit transcendental Colebrook Equation:
1√f = -2.0 · log10 ( ε / D3.7 + 2.51Re · √f )

Key Concepts

Research and Extraction by Wong Kah Mun

Real and Complex Roots

A root-finding problem means finding the value of x that makes a function equal to zero: f(x) = 0. The value of x is called the root of the equation.

Real Root Example:
x2 - 4 = 0 ⇒ (x - 2)(x + 2) = 0 ⇒ x = 2,    x = -2
Complex Root Example (using Imaginary Unit i2 = -1):
x2 + 4 = 0 ⇒ x = ±2i

Analytical vs. Numerical Guesses

Example of Analytical Quadratic Solution:
x2 - 5x + 6 = 0
(x - 2)(x - 3) = 0 ⇒ x = 2,    x = 3
The roots of this simple equation are found exactly.
Example of Complex Quadratic Solution:
x2 + 2x + 5 = 0
x = -2 ± √(2)2 - 4(1)(5) 2(1) = -2 ± √-16 2 = -1 ± 2i

Why Muller's Method?

Standard methods (Bisection, Fixed-Point, Newton's, Secant, False Position) can only solve for real roots. When a complex root is acquired, we need to use Muller's Method. However, Muller's method can also solve real roots efficiently. Unlike Newton's, it does not require computing derivatives, making it easier to apply to complex functions.

Step-by-Step Implementation

Research and Extraction by Lim Wei Hen (Henry)

Muller's method approximates a root by fitting a quadratic polynomial (parabola) through three points and calculating where it crosses the horizontal line.

Table 2: Algorithmic Symbols and Definitions
Symbol Definition
f(x)Nonlinear function whose root is sought
x₀, x₁, x₂Three initial approximations (starting values)
x₃New approximation of the root
h₀, h₁Distances between successive approximations
δ₀, δ₁First divided differences (slopes)
a, b, cCoefficients of the interpolating quadratic polynomial
DDiscriminant of the quadratic equation
EChosen optimized denominator

εDesired tolerance (error limit)

Detailed Algorithmic Steps

Step 1: Choose Three Initial Approximations

Select three starting values near the expected root: x0, x1, x2 and compute their corresponding function values: f(x0), f(x1), f(x2). Three points are required because a unique quadratic polynomial can be constructed through exactly three points.

Step 2: Compute the Interval Lengths

Calculate the horizontal spacings. These quantities measure the distances between successive approximations:

h0 = x1 - x0,      h1 = x2 - x1
Step 3: Compute the First Divided Difference

Calculate the slopes of the secant lines. These describe how rapidly the function changes between the selected points:

δ0 = f(x1) - f(x0) h0 ,      δ1 = f(x2) - f(x1) h1
Step 4: Construct the Quadratic Interpolating Polynomial

The fitted parabola serves as a local approximation and is written as P(x) = a(x - x2)2 + b(x - x2) + c, with coefficients solved as:

a = δ1 - δ0 h1 + h0 ,      b = ah1 + δ1,      c = f(x2)
Step 5: Compute the Discriminant

Compute the discriminant, which determines the roots of the approximating parabola. If b2 - 4ac < 0, D turns complex, allowing the algorithm to naturally locate complex conjugate solutions:

D = √b2 - 4ac
Step 6: Choose the Larger Denominator

The denominator parameter E is selected with the larger magnitude. This is one of the most critical steps in Muller's method, specifically formulated to prevent subtractive cancellation and enhance numerical stability:

E = { b + D,    if |b + D| > |b - D| b - D,    if |b - D| ≥ |b + D|
Step 7 & 8: Compute the Correction and New Approximation

Determine the increment correction h, which indicates how far the next estimate should shift from x2, and update the approximation:

h = - 2c E ,      x3 = x2 + h

Real-World Application

Research by Loh Eng Tom, Wong Kah Mun, and Lim Wei Hen

Drone Delivery Obstacle Avoidance

Calculating UAV Minimum Safety Clearance

In modern logistics, autonomous delivery drones (UAVs) must navigate safely through dense urban corridors. To prevent crash incidents, the flight control computer must compute the safe clearance distance from high-rise buildings in real time. Suppose a drone encounters a dynamic boundary obstacle. The critical clearance distance x (in meters) between the drone and the structure is mathematically modeled by the cubic relation:
P(x) = x3 - 250x2 + 15,000x - 200,000 = 0
The smallest positive real root (x1) represents the critical minimum safety boundary. Finding this root enables the flight system to adjust its vector trajectory smoothly without slowing down.

Step-by-Step Numerical Calculations

Based on dynamic radar telemetry, the root is known to reside between 10 and 20 meters. We select the following starting values:
x0 = 10.0,    x1 = 15.0,    x2 = 20.0
Evaluating function outputs:
P(10.0) = (10.0)3 - 250(10.0)2 + 15,000(10.0) - 200,000 = -74,000.00
P(15.0) = (15.0)3 - 250(15.0)2 + 15,000(15.0) - 200,000 = -27,875.00
P(20.0) = (20.0)3 - 250(20.0)2 + 15,000(20.0) - 200,000 = 8,000.00
Iteration 1 Calculations:

i. Differences & Slopes: h₀ = 5.0, h₁ = 5.0, slopes δ₀ = 9225.00, δ₁ = 7175.00.

ii. Parabola Coefficients: a = -205.00, b = 6150.00, c = 8000.00.

iii. Discriminant: D = √44,382,500 = 6662.0192.

iv. Denominator: E = b + √D = 12812.0192.

v. Next Guess: x₃ = 20.0 - 16,000 / 12812.0192 = 18.751173.

Discussions and Interpretations

I. Critical Safety Margin: The computed root rigorously proves that the absolute minimum safe clearance distance between the delivery drone and the high-rise building is 18.76 meters (rounded to two decimal places). If the UAV's trajectory planning algorithm allows it to fly any closer than this threshold, it risks entering the catastrophic collision boundary or experiencing aerodynamic turbulence from the structure [7].

II. Algorithmic Efficiency and Zero-Latency Processing: In real-time drone navigation, onboard flight computers must make decision changes within milliseconds to avoid sudden obstacles. Unlike open methods like Newton's method, which require continuous evaluation of analytical derivatives (P '(x)), Muller's method operates completely derivative-free. By utilizing three dynamic sensor points to construct sequential parabolas, it naturally mirrors the curvature of the safety corridor, dropping the true relative error significantly from 0.0325% in Iteration 1 to a perfect 0.0000% by Iteration 3 [6].

III. Smooth Flight Trajectory Correction: Because Muller's method achieves an incredibly low absolute error of just 0.0061 in its very first iteration, the flight controller can instantly lock onto the safe path far in advance. This extreme numerical stability prevents the drone from making sharp, jerky emergency braking maneuvers, thereby conserving significant battery life and maintaining high operational stability for urban logistics [7].
