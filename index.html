Study on Muller's Method

An Analysis and Implementation of Numerical Solutions for Real and Complex Roots

1. Background

What is a Root-Finding Problem?

A root-finding problem involves finding the values of $x$ (roots) that satisfy the equation: $$f(x) = 0$$ where $f(x)$ is a given continuous function. These roots can be either real or complex numbers.

History of Root-Finding

Root-finding is one of the oldest mathematical challenges. Ancient civilizations, such as the Babylonians, had methods to approximate square roots (essentially solving $x^2 - A = 0$). Over the centuries, mathematicians like Isaac Newton, Joseph Raphson, and Thomas Simpson developed iterative methods. In the 20th century, with the advent of computers, complex root-finding algorithms like Muller's Method (developed by David E. Muller in 1956) were introduced to handle non-linear and polynomial equations where analytical solutions are impossible to find.

Examples of Application

  • Physics & Astronomy: Calculating planetary orbits using Kepler’s Equation.
  • Engineering: Analyzing mechanical vibrations, fluid flows (e.g., Colebrook equation for pipe friction), or heat transfer.
  • Finance: Calculating the Internal Rate of Return (IRR) where future cash flows are equated to present value.

Importance of Root-Finding Problems

Many real-world systems are governed by non-linear relationships. Since analytical algebraic formulas only exist for equations of degree 4 or lower (Abel-Ruffini theorem), numerical approximation methods are essential tools for scientists and engineers to analyze complex processes.

2. Key Concepts

How to Solve Root-Finding Problems?

Root-finding problems can be solved in two ways:

  • Analytically: Finding exact mathematical expressions for roots (e.g., using algebraic formulas).
  • Numerically: Using iterative processes to approximate the roots step-by-step to a specified tolerance level.

Types of Roots

  • Real Roots: The point where the graph of $f(x)$ intersects the x-axis. Example: For $x^2 - 5x + 6 = 0$, the roots are $x = 2$ and $x = 3$.
  • Complex Roots: Conjugate pairs that occur when the graph does not cross the x-axis. Example: For $x^2 + 4 = 0$, the roots are complex: $x = \pm 2i$.

Numerical Methods Overview

Traditional numerical methods like the Bisection method, Fixed-point iteration, Newton's method, Secant method, and the False Position method are highly effective, but they are generally limited to finding real roots.

When a function has complex roots, these standard methods fail unless initialized with complex values. Muller's Method was specifically designed to handle complex roots automatically, even when starting with real initial guesses. It does this by interpolating three points with a parabola rather than a straight line (as done in the Secant method).

Analytical vs. Numerical: When to Use Which?

  • Analytical: Preferred when the equation is simple, linear, quadratic, or highly symmetrical, providing an exact symbolic answer.
  • Numerical: Necessary when the equation contains mixed transcendental functions (e.g., $e^x - \ln(x) = 5$) or high-degree polynomials, where algebraic solutions do not exist.

Comparison of Methods for Real Roots

Method Type Convergence Rate Stability/Robustness Can find Complex Roots?
Bisection Bracketing Linear ($r = 1.0$) Very stable (always converges if brackets exist) No
Fixed-Point Open Linear ($r = 1.0$) Conditional (depends on derivative $|g'(x)| < 1$) No
Newton's Open Quadratic ($r = 2.0$) Sensitive to initial guess and $f'(x) = 0$ No (unless starting with complex guess)

Muller's Open Nearly Quadratic ($r \approx 1.84$) Good stability, handles inflection points well Yes (even with real guesses)

3. Step-by-Step Implementation of Muller's Method

Muller's method uses three initial approximations $x_0, x_1,$ and $x_2$ to construct a quadratic equation of the form $P(x) = a(x - x_2)^2 + b(x - x_2) + c$ that passes through $(x_0, f(x_0)), (x_1, f(x_1)),$ and $(x_2, f(x_2))$.

Step-by-step procedure:

  1. Define three initial guesses: $x_0, x_1, x_2$.
  2. Evaluate the function values: $f(x_0), f(x_1), f(x_2)$.
  3. Calculate the differences: $$h_0 = x_1 - x_0, \quad h_1 = x_2 - x_1$$ $$\delta_0 = \frac{f(x_1) - f(x_0)}{h_0}, \quad \delta_1 = \frac{f(x_2) - f(x_1)}{h_1}$$
  4. Compute coefficients for the quadratic interpolating polynomial: $$a = \frac{\delta_1 - \delta_0}{h_1 + h_0}$$ $$b = a \cdot h_1 + \delta_1$$ $$c = f(x_2)$$
  5. To avoid subtraction cancellation and minimize round-off errors, compute the next approximation $x_3$ using: $$x_3 = x_2 - \frac{2c}{b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}$$ Note: Choose the sign in the denominator that matches the sign of $b$ to make the denominator larger in absolute value.
  6. Check for convergence. If $|x_3 - x_2| < \epsilon$ (tolerance), stop. Otherwise, set $x_0 = x_1$, $x_1 = x_2$, $x_2 = x_3$, and repeat.

5. Real-world Application (No Exact Solution Needed)

Chemical Engineering: Van der Waals Equation of State

The non-ideal behavior of gases is modeled by the Van der Waals equation: $$\left( P + \frac{a}{v^2} \right) (v - b) = R T$$ When rearranged as a function of specific volume $v$, it becomes a cubic polynomial: $$f(v) = v^3 - \left(b + \frac{RT}{P}\right)v^2 + \frac{a}{P}v - \frac{ab}{P} = 0$$

Given parameters for Carbon Dioxide ($CO_2$):

  • $a = 3.592 \text{ L}^2\cdot\text{atm/mol}^2$
  • $b = 0.04267 \text{ L/mol}$
  • $R = 0.08206 \text{ L}\cdot\text{atm/(mol}\cdot\text{K)}$
  • At temperature $T = 300\text{ K}$ and Pressure $P = 40\text{ atm}$
Plugging in the constants, we get the equation to solve: $$f(v) = v^3 - 0.65812v^2 + 0.08980v - 0.00383 = 0$$

Numerical solution using Muller's Method:

Initial guesses: $v_0 = 0.1, v_1 = 0.5, v_2 = 1.0$

  • Iteration 1: $v_3 = 0.50912$
  • Iteration 2: $v_3 = 0.49321$
  • Iteration 3: $v_3 = 0.49124$

The physical volume occupied by the $CO_2$ gas is approximately $0.49124 \text{ L/mol}$.
