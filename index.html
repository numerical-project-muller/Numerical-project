Exploring Muller's Method

An advanced analysis, proof, and engineering application of parabolic interpolation for solving real and complex mathematical roots.

1. Background

What is a Root-Finding Problem?

In numerical mathematics, a root-finding problem involves finding values of $x = c$ (roots) that satisfy the equation $f(c) = 0$ for a continuous function $f(x)$. Geometrically, finding a root corresponds to identifying the exact point where the curve of the function intersects or touches the x-axis.

Why It Matters

Most real-world physical systems are governed by non-linear relationships. Since analytical algebraic formulas only exist for equations of degree 4 or lower, engineers rely heavily on robust numerical approximation methods.

Milestones in Root-Finding History

Ancient Babylonians Square Root Approximations First recorded steps in solving quadratic relations.
17th Century Newton-Raphson Method Introduced linear derivatives to achieve quadratic convergence for real roots.
1956 (Modern Era) David E. Muller's Method Introduced quadratic interpolation (parabolic fitting) to naturally resolve both real and complex conjugate roots.

2. Key Concepts

Analytical Approach

Exact Mathematical Solutions

Finding exact values algebraically. For example:

  • Real Root: $x^2 - 5x + 6 = 0 \implies x = 2, 3$
  • Complex Root: $x^2 + 4 = 0 \implies x = \pm 2i$

Numerical Approach

Approximate Iteration

Approximating roots step-by-step using algorithmic loops. While methods like Bisection and Newton's stay on the real axis, Muller's Method naturally handles complex numbers without needing complex initial guesses.

3. Step-by-Step Algorithm

Muller's method constructs a parabola passing through three points $(x_0, f(x_0)), (x_1, f(x_1)),$ and $(x_2, f(x_2))$. The next approximation $x_3$ is the intersection of this parabola with the x-axis.

1

Difference Formulas

Calculate the step intervals and slope rates: $$h_0 = x_1 - x_0, \quad h_1 = x_2 - x_1$$ $$\delta_0 = \frac{f(x_1)-f(x_0)}{h_0}, \quad \delta_1 = \frac{f(x_2)-f(x_1)}{h_1}$$

2

Parabolic Coefficients

Compute coefficients $a, b,$ and $c$ for the interpolating parabola: $$a = \frac{\delta_1 - \delta_0}{h_1 + h_0}$$ $$b = a \cdot h_1 + \delta_1, \quad c = f(x_2)$$

3

Root Correction

Calculate next point. Choose the sign in the denominator to maximize its absolute value: $$x_3 = x_2 - \frac{2c}{b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}$$

4. Mathematical Examples

Example 1: Comparing Newton's Method and Muller's Method

1. Problem Statement

Solve the following cubic polynomial equation to find its real root: $$f(x) = x^3 - 13x - 12 = 0$$ Analytical (Exact) Solution: By factoring the polynomial, we obtain $(x - 4)(x + 1)(x + 3) = 0$. Thus, the exact roots are $x_1 = -3, x_2 = -1,$ and $x_3 = 4$. In this example, we will trace and compare how both numerical methods converge toward the target exact root, $x = 4$.

2. Solution by using Newton's Method

Let $x_0 = 5.0$, and the derivative is $f'(x) = 3x^2 - 13$. The Newton's Recurrence Formula is: $$x_{n+1} = x_n - \frac{f(x_n)}{f'(x_n)} = x_n - \frac{x_n^3 - 13x_n - 12}{3x_n^2 - 13}$$

Iteration 1: Substitute $x_0 = 5.0$ into the function and its derivative:

$f(5.0) = (5.0)^3 - 13(5.0) - 12 = 48$

$f'(5.0) = 3(5.0)^2 - 13 = 62$

Apply the formula to find $x_1$: $x_1 = 5.0 - \frac{48}{62} = 4.225806$

Iteration 2: Substitute $x_1 = 4.225806$ into the formula:

$f(4.225806) = (4.225806)^3 - 13(4.225806) - 12 = 8.526602$

$f'(4.225806) = 3(4.225806)^2 - 13 = 40.572321$

Calculate $x_2$: $x_2 = 4.225806 - \frac{8.526602}{40.572321} = 4.015648$

3. Solution Using Muller's Method

Let $x_0 = 3.0, x_1 = 3.5, x_2 = 4.5$. Corresponding function values are: $$f(x_0) = (3.0)^3 - 13(3.0) - 12 = -24$$ $$f(x_1) = (3.5)^3 - 13(3.5) - 12 = -14.625$$ $$f(x_2) = (4.5)^3 - 13(4.5) - 12 = 20.625$$

Iteration 1 Step-by-Step:

i. Calculate differences ($h_0, h_1$) and slopes ($\delta_0, \delta_1$):

$h_0 = x_1 - x_0 = 0.5$

$h_1 = x_2 - x_1 = 1.0$

$\delta_0 = \frac{f(x_1) - f(x_0)}{h_0} = \frac{-14.625 + 24}{0.5} = 18.75$

$\delta_1 = \frac{f(x_2) - f(x_1)}{h_1} = \frac{20.625 + 14.625}{1.0} = 35.25$

ii. Compute coefficients ($a, b, c$) for the parabola:

$a = \frac{\delta_1 - \delta_0}{h_1 + h_0} = \frac{35.25 - 18.75}{1.0 + 0.5} = 11.00$

$b = a h_1 + \delta_1 = 11.00(1.0) + 35.25 = 46.25$

$c = f(x_2) = 20.625$

iii. Evaluate the discriminant ($D$):

$D = b^2 - 4ac = 46.25^2 - 4(11.0)(20.625) = 1231.5625 \implies \sqrt{D} = \sqrt{1231.5625} = 35.0936$

iv. Maximize the denominator:

Denominator $= b + \sqrt{D} = 46.25 + 35.093625 = 81.343625$

v. Calculate the next approximation ($x_3$):

$x_3 = x_2 - \frac{2c}{\text{Denominator}} = 4.5 - \frac{2(20.625)}{81.343625} = 3.992892$

4. Discussions and Interpretations for Example 1

4.1 Convergence Rate and Error-Decay Efficiency: Based on the empirical numerical data consolidated in Table 2 and Table 3, both Newton's method and Muller's method successfully converged to the exact analytical root $x = 4.000000$. However, their internal error-decay dynamics exhibit a striking contrast in terms of speed and efficiency.

Newton's method commenced with a substantial true relative error of $25.000\%$ at the initial guess $x_0 = 5.0$. Iterating through linear tangent updates, the true relative error dropped to $5.645\%$ in Iteration 1, and required 4 full iterations to completely eliminate the approximation error down to $0.000\%$.

In contrast, Muller's method displayed a significantly more aggressive convergence trajectory. Starting from three moving initial approximations ($x_0 = 3.0, x_1 = 3.5, x_2 = 4.5$), its very first iteration successfully slashed the true relative error down to an astonishing $0.177\%$ ($x_3 = 3.992892$). By Iteration 3, Muller's method achieved perfect convergence to $4.000000$ with a true relative error of $0.000\%$, effectively outpaced Newton's method by one full iteration.

4.2 Mathematical Interpretation of Algorithm Mechanisms: The structural reason behind Muller's superior error-clearing capacity lies in the geometric interpolation alignment of the two algorithms:

  • Newton's Method (Linear Tangent Approximation): This open method relies on a first-order Taylor series expansion, constructing a straight tangent line from a single localized point to project the next root. While it guarantees quadratic convergence ($r = 2.0$) when initialized sufficiently close to the root, its efficiency lags if the initial guess behaves poorly or carries an inflection drift relative to the function's global behavior.
  • Muller's Method (Quadratic Parabolic Approximation): This method utilizes three sequential data points to construct a unique moving quadratic polynomial: $$P(x) = a(x - x_2)^2 + b(x - x_2) + c$$ Because our target function $f(x) = x^3 - 13x - 12$ is a cubic polynomial, a curved parabolic interpolation naturally accommodates and mirrors the local curvature of the function significantly tighter than a straight linear tangent line ever can.

This geometric advantage mathematically justifies why Muller's method yielded an absolute error of merely $0.007108$ in its opening step. It empirically verifies that Muller's theoretical near-quadratic superlinear convergence rate ($r \approx 1.84$) can match or exceed Newton's method when solving polynomial systems, all while operating completely derivative-free (eliminating the need to evaluate $f'(x)$).

Complex Root Core Feature

Example 3: $f(x) = x^2 + x + 1 = 0$

Exact Roots: $x = -0.5 \pm 0.866025i$. This exemplifies Muller's primary selling point over traditional methods.

Why Bracketing & Newton's Fail:

Bracketing methods require a sign change ($f(a)f(b) < 0$). However, since $f(x) = (x+0.5)^2 + 0.75 > 0$ for all real $x$, the parabola never intersects the real x-axis, making brackets impossible.

Similarly, Newton's and Secant methods initialized with real parameters are restricted to the real number line due to real-valued derivatives and slopes.

How Muller's Resolves This:

Muller's solver applies the quadratic root formula where the discriminant $D = b^2 - 4ac$ sits inside a square root. If $D < 0$, taking the square root automatically introduces the imaginary unit $i$, naturally steering the iterations into the complex plane even with purely real initial guesses.

Mathematical One-Step Convergence:

Setting initial real guesses: $x_0 = -1$, $x_1 = 0$, $x_2 = 1 \implies f(-1) = 1, f(0) = 1, f(1) = 3$. $$a = 1.0, \quad b = 3.0, \quad c = 3.0$$ $$D = b^2 - 4ac = 9 - 12 = -3 \implies \sqrt{D} = \sqrt{3}i \approx 1.732051i$$ Choosing the positive denominator: $\text{Denom} = 3 + 1.732051i$. $$x_3 = 1 - \frac{6}{3 + \sqrt{3}i} = 1 - \frac{18 - 6\sqrt{3}i}{12} = -0.5 + 0.866025i$$

5. Real-World Application

Structural Vibration & Seismic Resonance Analysis

In structural engineering, analyzing the dynamic behavior of multi-story buildings subjected to seismic ground motions is a critical safety task. Consider a three-story shear frame building under lateral dynamic loading. To design against resonance, engineers must calculate the eigenvalues of the system to determine its natural frequencies. By formulating the characteristic determinant $\det(\mathbf{K} - \omega^2 \mathbf{M}) = 0$, where $\mathbf{M}$ is the lumped mass matrix and $\mathbf{K}$ is the structural stiffness matrix, and letting $\lambda = \omega^2$, we obtain the following cubic polynomial governing the structural frequency: $$P(\lambda) = \lambda^3 - 250\lambda^2 + 15000\lambda - 200000 = 0$$ We need to find the fundamental eigenvalue (the smallest positive real root) to prevent structural damage.

Iterative Process (Guesses: $\lambda_0 = 10, \lambda_1 = 15, \lambda_2 = 20$):

  • Iteration 1: Intervals $h_0 = 5, h_1 = 5$. Slopes $\delta_0 = 9225, \delta_1 = 7175$. Polynomial values $P(10) = -74000, P(15) = -27875, P(20) = 8000$. $$a = -205.000000, \quad b = 6150.000000, \quad c = 8000.000000$$ $$D = b^2 - 4ac = 44382500 \implies \sqrt{D} \approx 6661.996728$$ Choosing the positive denominator: $\text{Denom} = 12811.996728 \implies \lambda_3 \approx 18.751167 \quad (\epsilon_a = 6.66\%)$.
  • Iteration 2: Updated values: $\lambda_0 = 15$, $\lambda_1 = 20$, $\lambda_2 = 18.751167 \implies P(18.751167) \approx -47.753800$. Repeating the algorithm yields: $$a \approx -194.811000, \quad b \approx 6687.521000, \quad c \approx -47.753800$$ $$\lambda_3 \approx 18.758312 \quad (\epsilon_a = 0.038\%)$$
