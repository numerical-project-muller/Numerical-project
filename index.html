Background of Muller's Method

Research and Extraction by SingJie Srisoi

What is the root-finding problem?

The root-finding problem is a fundamental mathematical and algorithmic challenge in numerical analysis. Given a continuous function f(x), the goal is to determine a value x = c, called the root or zero of the function such that:
f(c) = 0
Geometrically, finding a root corresponds to identifying the exact point where the curve of the function intersects or touches the x-axis.

Categorization of Roots

  1. Real Roots: The values of x where the function crosses the real number line x-axis.
  2. Complex Roots: The values involving imaginary numbers where the function does not intersect the real x-axis, but possesses solutions in the complex plane.

Analytical vs. Numerical Approaches

  • Analytical Solutions: Simple algebraic functions can be solved exactly using traditional algebraic formulas.
  • Numerical Solutions: Higher-degree polynomials (degree more than and equal to 5) and transcendental functions cannot be solved using exact algebraic formulas. Therefore, we must rely on iterative numerical methods to approximate the roots within a specific margin of error.

History of Root-Finding Problem

The evolution of root-finding spans millennia, tracking a major structural shift from exact geometric formulas to advanced computational approximations.

Ancient and Medieval Foundations (c. 1800 BCE - 1500 CE) The Babylonians developed some of the earliest recorded numerical workflows, using primitive arithmetic to iteratively compute square roots for agricultural planning and land surveying. Early Asian and Mediterranean mathematics utilized basic linear interpolation techniques, such as the Method of False Position (Regula Falsi) to approximate solutions to simple practical linear equations.
The Algebraic Barrier (16th - 19th Century) During the Italian Renaissance, Scipione del Ferro, Gerolamo Cardano, and Lodovico Ferrari achieved massive breakthroughs by discovering general algebraic formulas to solve cubic and quartic equations. However, Niels Henrik Abel and Évariste Galois rigorously proved via the Abel-Ruffini Theorem (1824) that it is logically impossible to solve polynomials of degree 5 or higher using standard radicals. This historic barrier permanently shifted mathematics toward constructing iterative numerical approximations.
The Calculus and Modern Computing Era Sir Isaac Newton and Joseph Raphson integrated differential calculus, creating the Newton-Raphson Method using first-order tangent lines to converge rapidly on a root. The Secant Method arose as a derivative-free alternative, drawing straight lines through two distinct initial data points.
David Muller and the Birth of Muller's Method (1956) With the advent of programmable electronic computers in the mid-20th century, scientists needed robust, automated algorithms capable of locating complex roots without requiring a derivative function. In 1956, American mathematician and computer scientist David E. Muller published a major breakthrough. Instead of drawing a straight line through two points like the Secant Method, Muller's Method fits a quadratic parabola through three points. By applying a modified version of the quadratic formula to find where this interpolating parabola crosses the axis, Muller's method established two major mathematical advantages: superlinear convergence (rate of approx. 1.84) and automated discovery of complex roots via negative discriminants.

Real-World Application of Root-Finding

1. Finance and Economics (Internal Rate of Return) To determine the Internal Rate of Return (IRR) of a multi-year corporate investment, financial analysts must find the exact discount rate r that forces the Net Present Value (NPV) of future fluctuating cash flows to equal exactly zero.
NPV = ∑t=0n Ct (1 + r)t = 0
2. Fluid Dynamics (Colebrook Equation) In civil and chemical pipeline design, determining the turbulent fluid friction factor f requires solving the implicit transcendental Colebrook Equation:
1√f = -2.0 · log10 ( ε / D3.7 + 2.51Re · √f )
3. Advanced Statistics & Data Science Fitting a probability distribution to empirical datasets requires maximizing a specialized likelihood function. Statisticians calculate the first derivative of the log-likelihood function (the score function) and utilize root-finding subroutines to pinpoint the parameters where the gradient reaches zero.
4. Actuarial Science & Demographics Actuaries rely heavily on non-linear survival equations and population decay curves to assess life expectancy risks and price corporate insurance premiums effectively. Calculating risk thresholds or matching samples to theoretical distributions relies directly on root-finding algorithms.

Key Concepts

Research and Extraction by Wong Kah Mun

Real and Complex Roots

A root-finding problem means finding the value of x that makes a function equal to zero: f(x) = 0. The value of x is called the root of the equation.

1. Real Root: A real root is a solution that can be shown on the number line. For example:
x2 - 4 = 0 ⇒ (x - 2)(x + 2) = 0 ⇒ x = 2,    x = -2
2. Complex Root: A complex root contains an imaginary number, usually involving i, where i2 = -1. For example:
x2 + 4 = 0 ⇒ x = ±2i

Analytical vs. Numerical Approaches

Example of Quadratic Equation with Real Roots:
x2 - 5x + 6 = 0
(x - 2)(x - 3) = 0 ⇒ x = 2,    x = 3
This equation has real roots. We solve analytically when the equation is simple and can be solved exactly using algebra, factorization, or formulas.
Example of Quadratic Equation with Complex Roots:
x2 + 2x + 5 = 0
x = -2 ± √(2)2 - 4(1)(5) 2(1) = -2 ± √-16 2 = -1 ± 2i
Using the quadratic formula, we easily find that this equation has complex roots.

Analytical vs. Numerical: When to Use Which?

We solve analytically when the equation is simple and can be solved exactly. We solve numerically when the equation is complicated, cannot be factorized easily, or has no simple exact solution. For example, x3 - x - 1 = 0 is more suitable to be solved using numerical methods such as Newton’s Method, Secant Method, or Muller’s Method.

While Bisection, Fixed-Point, Newton's, Secant, and False Position can only solve for real roots, Muller's Method fits a quadratic curve through three points and does not require a derivative. It is highly useful when the root of an equation is difficult to find using simple algebra and can naturally solve for both real and complex roots.

Step-by-Step Implementation

Research and Extraction by Lim Wei Hen (Henry)

Given a nonlinear equation f(x) = 0, Muller's Method approximates a root by fitting a quadratic polynomial (parabola) through three known points and then determining where the parabola intersects the x-axis. Unlike Newton's Method, Muller's Method does not require the derivative f '(x) and it can converge to both real and complex roots.

Table 2: Algorithmic Symbols and Definitions
Symbol Definition
f(x)Function whose root is sought
x₀, x₁, x₂Three initial approximations (starting values)
x₃New approximation of the root
h₀Distance between x₀ and x₁
h₁Distance between x₁ and x₂
δ₀Slope between x₀ and x₁
δ₁Slope between x₁ and x₂
a, b, cCoefficients of the interpolating quadratic polynomial
DDiscriminant of the quadratic equation

εDesired tolerance (error limit)

Detailed Algorithmic Steps

Step 1: Choose three initial approximation

Choose three starting values near the expected root: x0, x1, x2 and evaluate the function at these points: f(x0), f(x1), f(x2). Three points are required because a unique quadratic polynomial can be constructed through exactly three points.

Step 2: Compute the interval length

Calculate the horizontal spacings, which measure the distances between successive approximations:

h0 = x1 - x0,      h1 = x2 - x1

Where: h0 is spacing between first and second points, and h1 is spacing between second and third points.

Step 3: Compute the First Divided Difference

Calculate the slopes of the secant lines, describing how rapidly the function changes between the selected points:

δ0 = f(x1) - f(x0) h0 ,      δ1 = f(x2) - f(x1) h1

Where: δ0 is the slope of the secant line through (x0, f(x0)) and (x1, f(x1)), and δ1 is the slope of the secant line through (x1, f(x1)) and (x2, f(x2)).

Step 4: Construct the Quadratic Interpolating Polynomial

The fitted parabola serves as a local approximation and is written as:

P(x) = a(x - x2)2 + b(x - x2) + c

Where:

a = δ1 - δ0 h1 + h0 ,      b = ah1 + δ1,      c = f(x2)

These coefficients define the parabola passing through the three points, serving as a local approximation of the original function.

Step 5: Compute the Discriminant

Compute the discriminant of the quadratic equation. The discriminant determines the roots of the approximating parabola. If b2 - 4ac < 0, then D becomes complex, allowing Muller's Method to naturally locate the complex roots:

D = √b2 - 4ac
Step 6: Choose the Larger Denominator

Calculate the denominator parameter E. The larger denominator is chosen to avoid subtractive cancellation and improve numerical stability. This is one of the most important steps in Muller's Method:

E = { b + D,    if |b + D| > |b - D| b - D,    if |b - D| ≥ |b + D|
Step 7 & 8: Compute the correction and the new approximation

Calculate the correction term h (which shows how far the next estimate should move from x2) and find the new root estimate:

h = - 2c E ,      x3 = x2 + h    (or generally   xn+1 = xn + h)

Because this is obtained from the root of the parabola rather than a straight secant line, it provides faster convergence.

Step 9: Check for stopping criterion

Check whether sufficient accuracy has been achieved:

|h| < ε      or      |f(x3)| < ε
Step 10: Update the approximation of points

If convergence has not been reached, shift the points and repeat Steps 2-9. The newest approximation becomes part of the next quadratic interpolation:

x0 ← x1,      x1 ← x2,      x2 ← x3

Discussions and Interpretations for Example 1:

4.1 Convergence Rate and Error-Decay Efficiency: Both Newton's method and Muller's method successfully converged to the exact analytical root x = 4.000000. Newton's method commenced with a substantial true relative error of 25.000% at the initial guess x0 = 5.0, dropping to 5.645% in Iteration 1, and requiring 4 full iterations to completely eliminate the approximation error down to 0.000%.

In contrast, Muller's method displayed a significantly more aggressive convergence trajectory. Starting from three moving initial approximations (x0 = 3.0, x1 = 3.5, x2 = 4.5), its very first iteration successfully slashed the true relative error down to an astonishing 0.177% (x3 = 3.992892). By Iteration 3, Muller's method achieved perfect convergence to 4.000000 with a true relative error of 0.000%, effectively outpacing Newton's method by one full iteration.

4.2 Mathematical Interpretation of Algorithm Mechanisms: The structural reason behind Muller's superior error-clearing capacity lies in the geometric interpolation alignment: Newton's Method relies on first-order tangent lines, whereas Muller's Method utilizes three sequential points to construct a moving quadratic polynomial P(x) = a(x - x2)2 + b(x - x2) + c. Because the target function is a cubic polynomial, curved parabolic interpolation naturally accommodates and mirrors the local curvature significantly tighter than a straight linear tangent line, allowing Muller's superlinear convergence rate (r ≈ 1.84) to match or exceed Newton's completely derivative-free.

Discussions and Interpretations for Example 3:

I. Why Traditional Methods Fail: Standard bracketing approaches (Bisection, False Position) rely strictly on the Intermediate Value Theorem, which requires finding a real interval [a, b] where the function changes signs (f(a) · f(b) < 0). However, since f(x) = x2 + x + 1 = 0 for all real numbers, the curve never crosses the x-axis, making real brackets mathematically impossible to establish. Similarly, open methods (Newton-Raphson, Secant) remain completely locked within the real number line if initialized with real numbers, because all derivative slopes and linear adjustments yield exclusively real values.

II. Spontaneous Complex Breakthrough: As proven in the step-by-step math section, the core algebraic formulation of Muller's method utilizes the square root of a discriminant (√(b2 - 4ac)). When b2 - 4ac < 0, the algorithm naturally handles negative numbers under the radical, introducing the imaginary component i. This allows it to break away from the real line and cross into the complex domain smoothly, even when started with purely real initial guesses (x0 = -1.0, x1 = 0.0, x2 = 1.0).

III. Exact One-Step Solution for Quadratic Systems: As observed in Table 6, the algorithm reaches the exact root with 0.000% error in exactly one iteration. This instantaneous convergence happens because Muller's method approximates functions by fitting a unique quadratic polynomial P(x) through three points. Since our target equation is already a quadratic function, the interpolating parabola matches the original function perfectly. Finding the roots of the parabola therefore yields the exact complex roots of the function instantly.

Drone Delivery Obstacle Avoidance

Calculating UAV Minimum Safety Clearance

In modern logistics, autonomous delivery drones (UAVs) must navigate safely through dense urban corridors. To prevent crash incidents, the flight control computer must compute the safe clearance distance from high-rise buildings in real time. Suppose a drone encounters a dynamic boundary obstacle. The critical clearance distance x (in meters) between the drone and the structure is mathematically modeled by the cubic relation:
P(x) = x3 - 250x2 + 15,000x - 200,000 = 0
The smallest positive real root (x1) represents the critical minimum safety boundary. Finding this root enables the flight system to adjust its vector trajectory smoothly without slowing down.

Step-by-Step Numerical Calculations

Based on dynamic radar telemetry, the root is known to reside between 10 and 20 meters. We select the following starting values:
x0 = 10.0,    x1 = 15.0,    x2 = 20.0
Evaluating function outputs:
P(10.0) = (10.0)3 - 250(10.0)2 + 15,000(10.0) - 200,000 = -74,000.00
P(15.0) = (15.0)3 - 250(15.0)2 + 15,000(15.0) - 200,000 = -27,875.00
P(20.0) = (20.0)3 - 250(20.0)2 + 15,000(20.0) - 200,000 = 8,000.00
Iteration 1 Calculations:

i. Differences & Slopes: h₀ = 5.0, h₁ = 5.0, slopes δ₀ = 9225.00, δ₁ = 7175.00.

ii. Parabola Coefficients: a = -205.00, b = 6150.00, c = 8000.00.

iii. Discriminant: D = √44,382,500 = 6662.0192.

iv. Denominator: E = b + √D = 12812.0192.

v. Next Guess: x₃ = 20.0 - 16,000 / 12812.0192 = 18.751173.

Discussions and Interpretations

I. Critical Safety Margin: The computed root rigorously proves that the absolute minimum safe clearance distance between the delivery drone and the high-rise building is 18.76 meters (rounded to two decimal places). If the UAV's trajectory planning algorithm allows it to fly any closer than this threshold, it risks entering the catastrophic collision boundary or experiencing aerodynamic turbulence from the structure.

II. Algorithmic Efficiency and Zero-Latency Processing: In real-time drone navigation, onboard flight computers must make decision changes within milliseconds to avoid sudden obstacles. Unlike open methods like Newton's method, which require continuous evaluation of analytical derivatives (P'(x)), Muller's method operates completely derivative-free. By utilizing three dynamic sensor points to construct sequential parabolas, it naturally mirrors the curvature of the safety corridor, dropping the true relative error significantly from 0.0325% in Iteration 1 to a perfect 0.0000% by Iteration 3.

III. Smooth Flight Trajectory Correction: Because Muller's method achieves an incredibly low absolute error of just 0.0061 in its very first iteration, the flight controller can instantly lock onto the safe path far in advance. This extreme numerical stability prevents the drone from making sharp, jerky emergency braking maneuvers, thereby conserving significant battery life and maintaining high operational stability for urban logistics.
